# Slide 4: Defining Intelligence — Part 1

- Header: **1. Definition**
- Main question: **What is Intelligence?**
- Dictionary Definition (Duden):
 - **German**: *Fähigkeit [des Menschen], abstrakt und vernünftig zu denken und daraus zweckvolles Handeln abzuleiten*
 - **English Translation**: *Ability [of human beings], to think abstract as well as reasonable and derive purposeful acting out of that*
- Core Questions & Answers:
 - **Can we Build Machines as Intelligent as we are?** *(Posed with a question mark)*
 - **Can we Promote Intelligence?** -> **Yes.**

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide initiates the first major section of the agenda by evaluating the traditional, formal definition of intelligence.

- By citing the **Duden** (the standard dictionary authority for the German language), the speaker establishes a rigid baseline. According to this definition, intelligence relies on a three-part pipeline: **abstract/logical thinking**, **rational processing**, and **purposeful execution (action)**.
- The slide explicitly highlights that traditional definitions structurally gate intelligence as an **"ability [of human beings]"**. This anthropocentric boundary serves as the primary hurdle when discussing AI.
- The speaker follows this with a pivotal existential question for the field of computer science: Can we replicate this exact human capacity within a machine?
- The final query, "Can we Promote Intelligence? Yes," suggests that intelligence is not static. It can be optimized, trained, and cultivated, hinting at why machine learning and systemic engineering are valid paths forward.

### Why this matters

- This slide exposes the semantic problem of AI: historical human language definitions inherently tie "intelligence" to biological humanity. To build Strong AI, engineering must either expand the definition or prove a digital machine can fulfill every condition of the human definition (abstract thought, reasoning, and purposeful action).
- The transition from a rigid definition to the prompt "Can we Promote Intelligence? Yes" signals a pragmatic shift from philosophy to execution.

### Interpretive note

- The question mark following "Can we Build Machines as Intelligent as we are?" leaves the ultimate conclusion open for the workshop participants to evaluate as the slides progress.
- Notice the stark contrast between the open-ended question about *building* human-level machine intelligence versus the definitive **"Yes"** given to *promoting* intelligence. This hints that while Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) remains a complex boundary, enhancing operational or algorithmic intelligence is an absolute reality.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide explores the third point of the agenda, unpacking the structural architecture of **Interaction**. The entire model is built to support the axiom: "No Interaction, no Intelligence!"

- The speaker uses classical Latin roots to map out a timeless cognitive pipeline: **Cognitio** (acquiring knowledge/perception), **Cogitation** (the internal act of thinking/re-evaluating), and **Actio** (the execution of a goal-oriented deed).

### Why this matters

- This slide provides a comprehensive, end-to-end cognitive architecture for AI. It demonstrates that intelligence cannot be an isolated brain in a vat; it must exist within a closed loop that continuously receives input from an environment and alters that environment through action.
- By placing **Information and Knowledge** at the bottom as a supporting layer rather than the main event , the slide argues that static database knowledge is useless without the active dynamic processes of recognition, problem-solving, and expression.

### Interpretive note

- This diagram elegantly visualizes how the Duden dictionary definition from Slide 5 (abstract thinking leading to purposeful action) is practically engineered into a functional system.
- Notice how **Learning** and **Solving** are centered as the bridges between perception and action. This architecture implies that modern LLMs or standard software might handle "Speech" or "Recognition", but true Strong AI requires the seamless integration of all these components simultaneously inside a continuous interaction loop.
