# Slide 6: Entity — Definition and Delimiters

- Header: **1. Entity**
- Core Definition: 
  - **Entity A** is *delimitable* and *atomic* (undividable).
- Core Questions & Answers:
  - **What Characterises an Entity in Principle?** $\rightarrow$ *It is delimitable.*
  - **What are Delimiters?** $\rightarrow$ *e.g., surfaces, different perceptions, ...*
  - **What is a Physical Entity?** $\rightarrow$ *A conglomerate of matter and waves.*
  - **What is a Mental Entity?** $\rightarrow$ *A representation.*

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide rigorously defines what makes a "thing" a thing. 

The fundamental requirement for any entity to exist is **delimitability**. If you cannot draw a boundary (a delimiter) around it to separate it from the rest of the universe, it is not an entity. 
- In the physical world, delimiters are usually physical properties (like the surface of a table). A physical entity is just a bordered chunk of matter/waves.
- In the mental world, a delimiter is a conceptual boundary. A mental entity is an internal representation of that bordered chunk.
Once bordered, the entity is considered "atomic." For the purposes of processing, the mind treats it as a single, indivisible unit.

### Why this matters

For computer vision and object detection models (like YOLO), "delimitability" is the entire game. An AI camera looking at a room just sees a massive grid of pixels. It must be programmed to find "surfaces" and "different perceptions" (edges, contrast, color changes) to draw a bounding box (a delimiter) around a group of pixels. Only once it draws that box has it successfully created an "Entity" that it can process further. Without delimiters, an AI cannot parse the world.

# Slide 14: Association — Counterpart Paradigm

- Header: **2. Association**
- Subtitle: **Counterpart paradigm**
- Core Rules of Association:
  1. If we link two entities mentally, we always assign compatible roles to each entity and get a role pair for that association.
  2. Roles are compatible if the role of one entity tells what that entity is for the other entity, and vice versa.
  3. Each role represents the action/behavior expectation of its entity.
  4. The two roles of one association are always counterparts in a sense that their action or behaviour is usually not contradictory or dead locking – they fit together.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines the rigorous mathematics of an "Association." An association is not just a dumb line drawn between two nodes. It is a dual-directional relationship governed by the "Counterpart Paradigm."

When Entity A connects to Entity B, both entities are assigned a "Role" (a behavioral expectation). Crucially, these roles must be perfectly symmetrical counterparts. If Entity A is a "Thrower," Entity B must be a "Catcher." If they don't fit perfectly together without dead-locking, the association fails. 

### Why this matters

In software engineering, this is the definition of **Foreign Keys** and **Bidirectional Relationships** in database design. If Table A has a `One-to-Many` relationship with Table B, then Table B must have a `Many-to-One` relationship with Table A. The counterpart paradigm ensures data integrity across complex cognitive networks.

  4. Cornelia has the mother role in that relation.
  5. Julia has the daughter role in that relation.
  6. The two roles mother and daughter are counterparts.
  7. The role pair mother&daughter is the type of the association that links Cornelia and Julia.

Cornelia as an atomic entity is just a person; she only becomes a "Mother" when the edge to "Julia" is drawn.

### Why this matters

This is a masterclass in **Semantic Triples (Subject-Predicate-Object)** used in Knowledge Graphs (like RDF/SPARQL). To teach an AI to understand human families, you must code these exact 10 assertions into its logic engine. The AI must understand that `<Cornelia> <is_mother_of> <Julia>` automatically guarantees the inverse counterpart `<Julia> <is_daughter_of> <Cornelia>`, and that these roles carry specific behavioral expectations.

# Slide 21: Concept — Primitive, Native, and Derived

- Header: **4. Concept**
- Core Definitions:
  - **Primitive Concept**: A mental entity that stands for itself alone; it is not defined by any association schema (e.g., a pure "name" like Cornelia).
  - **Native Concept**: Defined by an association schema and is usually derived from the physical world (e.g., the specific counterpart link of `mother & daughter`).
  - **Derived Concept**: Has a network scheme built of *multiple* association schemas.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide establishes the three tiers of conceptual complexity.

1. **Primitive Concepts** are the raw, unlinked nodes. The name "Cornelia" means nothing on its own. It is just a static string of text holding a place in memory.
2. **Native Concepts** represent a single edge (association) between two nodes. The concept of "mother & daughter" is native because it perfectly mirrors a fundamental physical reality (biological reproduction). 
3. **Derived Concepts** are complex webs of multiple edges. (The next slides will likely show how multiple native concepts network together to create a derived concept like "Family" or "Brother & Sister").

### Why this matters

In software engineering, this maps perfectly to **Data Structures**. 
- A Primitive Concept is a primitive data type (String, Int). 
- A Native Concept is a simple Pair or Tuple (Key-Value pair). 
- A Derived Concept is a complex Class or Object containing nested relationships. An AI must be able to assemble primitives into native pairs, and native pairs into derived objects to process reality.

# Slide 23: Concept — Derived Concept

- Header: **4. Concept**
- Subtitle: **Derived Concept**
- Core Concept: 
  - The concept **brother & sister** is derived from a schema network of native concepts (*daughter & mother*, *daughter & father*, *son & mother*, *son & father*).
- Logic Structure (GREBTL):
  - `{<sister> (sister & brother) <brother>} (concept & schema)`
  - EQUALS: `{<sister> (daughter & mother) <mother> (mother & son) <brother> (son & father) <father> (father & daughter) <sister>}`

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide formally defines how the brain builds a "Derived Concept" using algebra.

It proves that the concept of "Brother & Sister" is an illusion. It is just a semantic shortcut for a much longer mathematical path. If you traverse the graph from `<sister>` up to `<mother>` and back down to `<brother>`, you have mathematically proven the "brother & sister" relationship. The brain just takes that long graphical loop and creates a new, direct associative edge ("brother & sister") to save processing time in the future.

The presenter introduces **GREBTL** (Generic Role-Entity-Based Thought Language) to show exactly how this traversal is calculated algebraically. 

### Why this matters

This is the exact mechanic of **Graph Traversals and Inferences** in AI. If you want an AI to understand human relationships, you don't need to manually program every possible combination. You only program the "native concepts" (parent/child). If the AI needs to figure out if X and Y are siblings, it runs a graph query (e.g., in SPARQL or Cypher) looking for a shared parent node. When the query succeeds, the AI "derives" the concept of sibling automatically.

First, they must define external edges. What are the Counterpart Roles for a horse? (e.g., If the horse is a "Mount," the counterpart is a "Rider." If the horse is "Prey," the counterpart is "Predator").
Second, they must define internal edges. They must create a graph of the horse's anatomy (Head connected to Neck, Neck to Torso, Torso to Legs), proving that even a single entity is actually just a nested concept made of smaller associated entities.

### Why this matters

This is exactly how you program a **Knowledge Graph** or a **3D Physics Rig**. If you want an AI to generate a video of a horse, it must have a semantic understanding of its external ecosystem (who rides it) and its internal skeletal rig (how the torso connects to the legs).

# Slide 28: Conceptualisation — Learning

  2. *Through repeating action patterns*

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines "Learning" purely as a process of schema acquisition. 

An entity isn't born with the schema for a horse or a brother. It has to acquire them. The presenter lists the four fundamental ways a brain writes a new schema to memory: direct physical experience, repetitive trial and error, combining old schemas into a new one (e.g., combining a horse and a horn to make a unicorn), or direct supervised injection by a teacher.

### Why this matters

For AI, this slide perfectly categorizes the four branches of **Machine Learning**:
1. **Experience**: Unsupervised Learning (finding patterns in raw data).
2. **Repeating Action Patterns**: Reinforcement Learning (trial and error against a reward).
3. **Transfer**: Transfer Learning (taking a model trained on one dataset and adapting it to another).
4. **Teachers**: Supervised Learning / Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

# Slide 31: Materialisation (Instantiation)

- Header: **6. Materialisation**
- Core Definition: 
  - **Materialisation (instantiation)** means to bring a concept into real life by:
    - Acting (physical world)
    - Thinking (mental world)
- Core Visual: 
  - Shows the abstract schema graph (`<sister>`, `<brother>`, `<father>`, `<mother>`).
  - A massive arrow labeled **"instantiate / materialise"** points downward.
  - The graph transforms: The abstract variable `<sister>` is replaced with the specific entity **Julia**. `<brother>` becomes **Caesarion**. `<father>` becomes **Julius**. `<mother>` becomes **Cornelia**.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide represents the final execution phase of intelligence. The mind holds abstract, empty templates (Concept Schemas). When the Actor decides to act, it must "Materialise" that abstract template into reality by plugging real, specific entities into the empty variables. 

In the visual, the abstract concept of a "family structure" is instantiated by plugging in the specific historical figures of Julius Caesar, Cornelia, and their children. Materialisation is the bridge between pure abstract math and concrete reality.

### Why this matters

In software engineering, this is the exact definition of **Object Instantiation**. A Class (the Concept Schema) is an abstract template written in code. You cannot interact with a Class. You must use a constructor to instantiate a specific Object from that Class (e.g., `Family myFamily = new Family("Julius", "Cornelia", "Julia", "Caesarion");`). The object exists in the active memory (real life) and can now execute actions.
