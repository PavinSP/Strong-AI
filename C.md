# Slide 38: The Paradox and Dualism Explained

- Header: **7. Conclusion**
- Section: **Paradox?**
- The Paradoxical Chain:
 - (a) Mental entities are part of the mental world.
 - (b) The mental world takes place in brains.
 - (c) Brains are part of the real world.
 - (d) **Therefore, Mental entities are part of the physical world.**
- Section: **Dualisms?**
- The Fundamental Dualities:
 - **Thoughts are PHYSICAL and MENTAL.**
 - **Our minds belong to ourself AND the world.**

### Conceptual Explanation

This final substantive slide exposes the core logical paradox underlying the entire Reality framework and proposes a resolution through dual-aspect thinking.

- **The Paradox**: Following logical steps (a)→(b)→(c)→(d), we arrive at a seemingly contradictory conclusion: mental entities, which feel purely internal and subjective, are simultaneously part of the physical world. How can pure thought be matter?
- **The Resolution via Dualism**: Rather than resolving the paradox by choosing one side (pure physicalism or pure idealism), the presenter proposes that thoughts are genuinely **both physical and mental**. They possess two aspects that are inseparable.
- **The Identity**: Similarly, our minds are simultaneously **ourself** (subjective, private, first-person) **and the world** (objective, public, third-person). We are both the observer and the observed.

### Why this matters

- For computer scientists building AGI, this dualism is liberating. It justifies why machines can be programmed (physical manipulation of silicon and electrons) and simultaneously exhibit intelligent, goal-directed behavior (mental processes). The paradox dissolves when both aspects are acknowledged as real.
- It addresses the original concern of Module C: to build a true digital brain, one does not need to "solve" consciousness; one needs to build systems that manifest both physical substrate properties and mental functional properties simultaneously.

### Interpretive note

- This is the moment where the presenter reveals his philosophical stance: he rejects both pure reductionism (everything is atoms) and pure idealism (everything is mind). He advocates for a **complementary dualism** where mind and matter are orthogonal aspects of a unified reality.
- For students trained in computer science, this is intellectually reassuring: your code is real (physical), and the ideas it implements are real (mental). No reconciliation is needed; both are true.
