# Slide 6: Actor — Prerequisites

- Header: **1. Actor**
- Subtitle: **Prerequisites**
- Core Requirements to be an Actor:
  1. **must be able to move** (Visual: Person + Hand symbol)
  2. **must be able to think** (Visual: Person + Brain symbol)
     - *no purposeful acting without anticipation*
     - *planning means abstracting*
     - *learn*
  3. **must be able to communicate** (except lone wolves) (Visual: Person + Speech waves symbol)

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines the exact hardware and software specifications required for an entity to be classified as an "Actor" capable of intelligent action.

1. **Mobility**: It must have an actuator (a hand, a motor, or write-access to a database). It must be able to change a state.
2. **Cognition**: It must have a brain/processor. Why? Because true "action" requires *anticipation* (predicting the future). To act purposefully, the actor must build a plan, which requires abstracting reality into an internal model. Furthermore, it must be able to learn from the results of its actions.
3. **Communication**: It must have a transmitter. The presenter notes an exception for "lone wolves" (entirely isolated systems), but generally, an actor must be able to move information to other actors to coordinate complex work.

### Why this matters

This is the literal design spec for an Agentic AI. 
- If an AI only has an LLM (thinking) but no API access to the outside world, it cannot *move*, so it is not an actor. 
- If a Boston Dynamics robot arm can move but cannot *anticipate or plan*, it is just a dumb tool, not an intelligent actor. 
- An AGI must possess all three prerequisites: actuators to move, logic models to plan/anticipate, and I/O channels to communicate.

# Slide 10: Physical Act — Purposes

- Header: **2. Physical Act**
- Core Classifications:
  1. **Move own body**
     - *1.a.) Physical*: Change own Position.
     - *1.b.) Mental*: Change others Mind (expression – body language) to set or generate information.
     - *1.c.) Physical and Mental*: Change Position to set/get Information.
  2. **Move other physical entity**
     - *2.a.) Physical*: Change others Position.
     - *2.b.) Mental*: Change others Mind (expression) to set or generate information.
     - *2.c.) Physical and Mental*: Change Position to set/get Information.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide deconstructs a physical movement based on its *purpose*, demonstrating that a physical act often has a mental goal.

When an actor moves their own body or moves another object, they are doing so for one of three reasons:
1. **Purely Physical**: Simply moving from A to B, or moving an object from A to B (e.g., walking, lifting a rock).
2. **Purely Mental**: Moving the body to change someone else's mind. This is body language or expression. Waving your hand doesn't physically push the other person, but the light bouncing off your hand enters their eyes and changes their internal information state.
3. **Hybrid**: Moving physically in order to acquire or set new information (e.g., walking around a corner specifically to *see* what is there).

### Why this matters

For robotics and AI, this defines the complexity of intention. A self-driving car turning its wheels (moving its body) isn't just a physical act to stay on the road (1.a). Sometimes it flashes its headlights to warn a human driver (1.b: changing a human's mind). An AI must understand the mental intent behind its physical outputs.

# Slide 13: Thinking Act — The Processing Loop

- Header: **4. Thinking Act**
- Core Definition: 
  - Processing of impressions, solving challenges, learning, planning and executing purposeful ACTIONS after determining a need for action.
- Visual Flow Cycle:
  - **Impressions** (Input) enter the mind.
  - They undergo **Perception** and **Recognition**.
  - This feeds into the core engines: **Solving**, **Learning**, **Information**, and **Knowledge**.

  - These commands exit the mind as **Expressions** (Output).

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide formally defines the "Thinking Act" as the central processing hub of intelligence. It sits between incoming Impressions and outgoing Expressions.

Thinking is not a singular event; it is a pipeline. First, the actor must process raw incoming data (perception/recognition). Then, it must route that data through its knowledge networks to solve challenges and learn. Finally, once a "need for action" is determined (the knowledge gap), the thinking act concludes by issuing outward commands (motion or speech) to execute a purposeful physical action. 

### Why this matters

For AI developers, this slide perfectly illustrates the architecture of a **Multi-Modal AI Agent**. 
- The left side (Impressions) is the Sensor layer (Computer Vision, Speech-to-Text).
- The center (Thinking) is the LLM / Logic Engine layer (RAG, vector databases, decision trees).
- The right side (Expressions) is the Actuator layer (Text-to-Speech, API calls, robotic controls). 
Thinking is simply the routing and processing of data between sensors and actuators.

# Slide 17: Speech Act — Purposes

- Header: **3 Speech Act** *(Note: The numbering jumps back to 3 here due to a typo in the original presentation)*
- Core Classifications:
  1. **Thoughtful (Deliberate Speech)**
     - *1.a.)* Change information of another Individuals by a declaration.
     - *1.b.)* Get information from another Individual by a question.
     - *1.c.) Physical*: Order an individual to manipulate physical entities.
  2. **Rash (Reflexive Speech)**
     - *2.a.)* Quick reaction to upsetting.
     - *2.b.)* Quick reaction to physical stimuli.
     - *2.c.)* Quick reaction to optical stimuli.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide categorizes the purpose of speech acts into two distinct buckets: "Thoughtful" (planned) and "Rash" (reflexive).

**Thoughtful speech** is strategic. The actor deliberately crafts a message to achieve a specific goal:
- To overwrite someone else's database (a declaration).
- To fill a knowledge gap in their own database (a question).
- To remotely control physical matter by ordering someone else to do it (a command).

**Rash speech** bypasses the deep planning loop. It is a biological or system reflex triggered immediately by an external stimulus (like shouting "Ouch!" when touching a hot stove, or a system throwing an automatic "Error!" log).

### Why this matters

In Natural Language Processing (NLP), this maps to Intent Classification. An intelligent chatbot must figure out if the user is making a declaration (storing a fact), asking a question (querying a database), or giving an order (triggering a function). Furthermore, AI systems themselves must be programmed to use these three tools to manage their own knowledge gaps and execute tasks in multi-agent swarms.

# Slide 20: Action Theory — Thesis

- Header: **5. Action Theory**
- Subtitle: **Thesis**
- Core Questions & Principles:
  - **What is this theory needed for?**
    - To explain why individuals do what they do.
    - In the social domain, it is for explaining how persons can live and work together.
  - **What is an action?**
    - Change in the physical or mental world.
    - Actions always create, change, or delete entities.
    - Action is always combined with motion/movement.
  - **Why and when is an action started?**
    - *When perceived impressions do not correspond to my inner values.*
  - **The Work System Framework** (Visual diagram):
    - Actions are framed by a work system consisting of: **Actor(s)** (Who), **Goal/Purpose** (Why), **Work Tool(s)** (With what), **Work Process** (How), and **Work Object(s)** (What).

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines the fundamental laws of Action Theory. 

First, it defines the physics of action: it is always a movement that creates, changes, or deletes an entity. 
Second, it provides the most important psychological/algorithmic rule in the seminar: **Why is an action started?** An action is *only* triggered when the agent's perception of the world does not match its internal target values (its goal state). 
Finally, it ties action back to the very first module of the course (Module A), reminding students that every action is a "Work System" requiring an Actor, a Tool, a Process, an Object, and a Goal.

### Why this matters

This is the exact definition of a **Control Loop** (like a PID controller or a Reinforcement Learning reward function). 
- An AI has a target value (e.g., "The room should be 70 degrees"). 
- It perceives the world (e.g., "The room is 80 degrees"). 
- Because the impression does not correspond to the inner value, the AI is triggered to *act* (turn on the AC) to change the physical world until the impression matches the value. This discrepancy is the source of all intelligent motivation.

# Slide 26: Action Plan — Thesis

- Header: **6. Action Plan**
- Subtitle: **Thesis**
- Core Principles:
  - Actions are always based on a plan.
  - A plan is produced in our mind by thinking.
  - A plan shows how to transform a discourse world from an initial state to a final state.
  - All single steps of motion and of speech are part of the action plan.
  - The processing of the plan is carried out by motion and speech.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines the exact nature and execution of an Action Plan. 

Crucially, it defines a plan as a mathematical transformation. The mind identifies an "initial state" (Current World) and calculates a trajectory to a "final state" (Wanted World). Every single atomic movement (a footstep) or spoken word is just a node on that calculated trajectory. The physical and speech acts are merely the "processing" (the rendering/execution) of the internal mental calculation.

### Why this matters

In computer science, this is identical to **State Space Search** in AI (like A* search algorithms or reinforcement learning policies). An AI represents the world as a state graph. The "Action Plan" is the optimal path found by the pathfinding algorithm to transition the system from the initial state to the target state. The actuators simply execute the steps along that path.
