# Slide 2: Repetition „Information“

- Header: **Repetition „Information“**
- Core Review Questions:
  - What is information?
  - Where is information?
  - How is information transferred from sender to receiver?
  - Can information be deleted and how?

### Conceptual Explanation

As is customary in this seminar, the presenter begins with a review of the previous module ("E Information") to ensure the foundational concepts are locked in before building upon them.

The questions recall the strict Body-Mind Dualism established earlier:
- **What/Where is it?** Information is a mental entity (a representation) that exists *only* inside a brain or processing engine. 
- **How is it transferred?** It cannot be transferred directly. It must be materialized into physical waves (communication), sent across a medium, and reconstructed by the receiver's impressors.
- **Can it be deleted?** Yes, by destroying the physical storage medium or reallocating the memory space, causing the mental representation to cease to exist.

This review sets the stage: an agent must use its internally stored information to dictate its external actions.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide outlines the structural roadmap for Module F, categorizing "Action" into distinct types. 

The presenter breaks action down into three primary verbs, all tied to the concept of a "Mover":
1. **Moving Things** (Physical Acts like lifting a box).
2. **Moving Thoughts** (Mental Acts like reasoning or updating a database).
3. **Moving Information** (Speech Acts like talking or sending data packets).

The prominent visual overlap of the "Intelligence" definition reminds students *why* they are studying action. Action is the second half of the intelligence equation. You cannot have "purposeful acting" (Intelligence) without a rigorous theory of what an action actually is.

### Why this matters

For AI developers, this agenda classifies the output layers of an AGI. 
- A robotic arm executing a motor command is a **Physical Act**.
- An LLM processing context and updating its internal attention weights is a **Mental Act**.
- An AI generating a text response or making an API call to another system is a **Speech Act**. 
All three are valid, measurable "Actions" that a system takes to change its environment.

# Slide 4: Workshop „Action“

- Header: **Workshop „Action“**
- Core Brainstorming Prompts:
  1. What is an action? Define that as precise and as abstract as possible. Think also of the work matrix we used in chapter A.
  2. Who can execute actions? Or what is needed to execute actions?
  3. Which different types and purposes of actions would you distinguish?
  4. Do you think, an action is the only connection between the mental and the physical world? Can you find other connections?

### Conceptual Explanation

This workshop challenges the students to define the physics and philosophy of "doing something." 

By asking them to refer back to the "work matrix" from Chapter A, the presenter is reinforcing that an action is synonymous with "work"—it requires the expenditure of energy to alter a state. It asks the audience to deduce the requirements of an actor (what hardware/software is needed to act?) and probes a profound philosophical question: Is action the *only* bridge between the mental realm (thoughts) and the physical realm (the world)? 

### Why this matters

For AI systems, defining "action" precisely is a prerequisite for safety and capability. If we don't know what constitutes an action, we cannot constrain an AI from taking dangerous actions. An AI writing a malicious script to its own memory (a mental act) might be just as dangerous as an AI moving a physical robotic arm.

# Slide 6: Actor — Prerequisites

- Header: **1. Actor**
- Subtitle: **Prerequisites**
- Core Requirements to be an Actor:
  1. **must be able to move** (Visual: Person + Hand symbol)
  2. **must be able to think** (Visual: Person + Brain symbol)
     - *no purposeful acting without anticipation*
     - *planning means abstracting*
     - *learn*
  3. **must be able to communicate** (except lone wolves) (Visual: Person + Speech waves symbol)

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide defines the exact hardware and software specifications required for an entity to be classified as an "Actor" capable of intelligent action.

1. **Mobility**: It must have an actuator (a hand, a motor, or write-access to a database). It must be able to change a state.
2. **Cognition**: It must have a brain/processor. Why? Because true "action" requires *anticipation* (predicting the future). To act purposefully, the actor must build a plan, which requires abstracting reality into an internal model. Furthermore, it must be able to learn from the results of its actions.
3. **Communication**: It must have a transmitter. The presenter notes an exception for "lone wolves" (entirely isolated systems), but generally, an actor must be able to move information to other actors to coordinate complex work.

### Why this matters

This is the literal design spec for an Agentic AI. 
- If an AI only has an LLM (thinking) but no API access to the outside world, it cannot *move*, so it is not an actor. 
- If a Boston Dynamics robot arm can move but cannot *anticipate or plan*, it is just a dumb tool, not an intelligent actor. 
- An AGI must possess all three prerequisites: actuators to move, logic models to plan/anticipate, and I/O channels to communicate. 

### Interpretive note

- The typo *"planing"* instead of *planning* is preserved from the original text. The icons visually align perfectly with the three types of acts listed on the agenda (Physical, Mental, Speech).

# Slide 7: Actor — Three Action Types

- Header: **1. Actor**
- Subtitle: **Three Action Types**
- Core Classifications:
  - **Outwards (External Actions)**:
    1. **Act by body**: Physical movement/manipulation (Icon: Person + Hand).
    2. **Act by speech**: Communication/transfer of information (Icon: Person + Speech waves).
  - **Inwards (Internal Actions)**:
    3. **Act by thinking**: Internal mental processing (Icon: Brain).

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide cleanly categorizes the three verbs established on the agenda into a directional framework. 

Actions are either directed **Outwards** at the physical environment (moving a box or speaking a command to someone else), or they are directed **Inwards** at the mental environment (thinking, reasoning, or storing memory). 

### Why this matters

For AI systems, this defines the boundaries between physical robotics and internal logic. 
- "Act by thinking" is entirely internal; it changes the state of the AI's own memory but doesn't immediately change the physical world. 
- "Act by body/speech" crosses the boundary from the mental world into the physical world, creating external consequences. An intelligent agent needs a control loop that carefully balances internal thinking before committing to an outward physical or speech act.

# Slide 8: Actor — Expression

- Header: **1. Actor**
- Subtitle: **Expression**
- Core Visual Metaphor:
  - Left side: A person "acting by body" and a person "acting by speech."
  - Right side: A massive, interconnected network of Physical Entities (people and objects interacting globally).
  - Overlaid: A giant Brain labeled "Information."

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide illustrates the consequence of the "Outward" actions defined on Slide 7. 

When an actor acts by body or by speech, they are executing an "Expression." This expression leaves the actor's isolated mental sandbox and enters the massive, shared network of the physical world. Their physical act or speech act creates a ripple effect, moving objects or transferring data to other nodes (people/machines) in the global network. 

The giant "Information" brain overlaid on the bottom suggests that the sum total of all these physical expressions and interactions is what generates the global pool of Information.

### Why this matters

In distributed AI systems (like swarm robotics or multi-agent LLM systems), this slide represents the network layer. When Agent A "acts by speech" (sends an API request), it alters the physical state of the network, which then becomes a new "Impression" for Agent B. Action is the engine that keeps the global network of information flowing.

# Slide 10: Physical Act — Purposes

- Header: **2. Physical Act**
- Core Classifications:
  1. **Move own body**
     - *1.a.) Physical*: Change own Position.
     - *1.b.) Mental*: Change others Mind (expression – body language) to set or generate information.
     - *1.c.) Physical and Mental*: Change Position to set/get Information.
  2. **Move other physical entity**
     - *2.a.) Physical*: Change others Position.
     - *2.b.) Mental*: Change others Mind (expression) to set or generate information.
     - *2.c.) Physical and Mental*: Change Position to set/get Information.

### Conceptual Explanation

This slide deconstructs a physical movement based on its *purpose*, demonstrating that a physical act often has a mental goal.

When an actor moves their own body or moves another object, they are doing so for one of three reasons:
1. **Purely Physical**: Simply moving from A to B, or moving an object from A to B (e.g., walking, lifting a rock).
2. **Purely Mental**: Moving the body to change someone else's mind. This is body language or expression. Waving your hand doesn't physically push the other person, but the light bouncing off your hand enters their eyes and changes their internal information state.
3. **Hybrid**: Moving physically in order to acquire or set new information (e.g., walking around a corner specifically to *see* what is there).

### Why this matters

For robotics and AI, this defines the complexity of intention. A self-driving car turning its wheels (moving its body) isn't just a physical act to stay on the road (1.a). Sometimes it flashes its headlights to warn a human driver (1.b: changing a human's mind). An AI must understand the mental intent behind its physical outputs.

# Slide 12: Workshop „Thinking Act“

- Header: **Workshop „Thinking Act“**
- Core Brainstorming Prompts:
  1. Why is thinking an action? What is thinking doing?
  2. Which „tools“ are used while thinking?
  3. What is changed by thinking?
  4. How can we determine a need for action?

### Conceptual Explanation

This workshop challenges the traditional assumption that "doing nothing but thinking" means no action is occurring. 

The presenter asks students to justify thinking as a form of labor. If thinking is an action, it must use tools (like logic gates, memory registers, or biological neurons) and it must *change* something. Since it doesn't change the physical world directly, it must change the internal mental world (updating schemas, creating new knowledge networks, or modifying action plans). 

The final question ("How can we determine a need for action?") bridges back to the Knowledge Gap. Thinking is the active process that continuously scans internal models for gaps and generates the motivation to act externally.

### Why this matters

For computer science, this is the definition of **Compute**. Running an algorithm uses electricity (tools/resources) to change the state of memory (what is changed). Even if a computer has no screen or robotic arms, it is still furiously acting internally. Understanding this is crucial for optimizing processing loops in an Agentic AI before it commits to external API calls.
